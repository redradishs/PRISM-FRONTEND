PRISM: Platform for Real-Time Intelligent Student Measurement System

Design and Development of an AI-Integrated Real-Time Online Assessment and Performance Monitoring System for Gordon College College of Computer Studies

Frederick S. Anicas — 202210761@gordoncollege.edu.ph

Menard Manlapaz — menardmanlapaz1@gmail.com

Denise Lou Punzalan — punzalan.deniselou@gordoncollege.edu.ph

College of Computer Studies, Gordon College, Olongapo City, Zambales, Philippines

Abstract

The educational landscape has undergone a significant shift toward technology-driven environments—a transition accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic. As institutions like Gordon College adapted to this rapid change, the reliance on diverse digital tools inadvertently created inefficiencies and "technostress" within the online assessment process. This study evaluates the impact of platform fragmentation on students and faculty at the College of Computer Studies (CCS) while assessing the readiness for PRISM.

Utilizing a quantitative descriptive-developmental research design, data was gathered through structured surveys from 298 students and 19 CCS instructors. Results highlighted significant concerns regarding academic integrity and cognitive load associated with switching between scattered third-party tools like Google Forms and Quizizz (overall weighted mean of 3.34).

Developed using the MEAN stack through Agile methodology, PRISM acts as a centralized solution featuring real-time proctoring with default browser tab-locking and automated, curriculum-based question generation utilizing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

Preliminary quantitative evaluations indicate high perceived usefulness (WM = 3.28) and technological readiness (WM = 3.34) among students. Furthermore, quantitative faculty validation confirmed the system's viability (OWM = 3.57), positioning PRISM as a highly feasible, unified solution that effectively reduces administrative workload while restoring strict assessment integrity.

1. The Problem: Platform Fragmentation & Technostress

Gordon College's approach to online assessments remains fragmented, with instructors relying on a mix of third-party tools rather than a standardized system. This lack of unified platform creates multiple challenges:

Cognitive Overload ("Technostress")

Students must constantly adapt to different interfaces and systems (Google Forms, Quizizz, Kahoot). This fragmentation imposes heavy cognitive load and negatively impacts academic performance and satisfaction.

Academic Integrity Concerns

Faculty express significant skepticism regarding online assessment validity. Unproctored systems are perceived as significantly easier to exploit and lack robust features to detect cheating.

Faculty Workload

Manually grading assessments and creating unique exams requires significant effort. Instructors struggle to provide specific feedback due to time required to verify results across multiple platforms.

Lack of Centralized Insights

No unified dashboard exists to track student performance. Assessment data remains scattered across disconnected systems, preventing targeted interventions and personalized support.

2. The Solution: PRISM

PRISM consolidates online assessment management into a single, unified platform that leverages Artificial Intelligence to streamline question generation, automate grading, and provide deep analytics while integrating intelligent monitoring features to detect and report cheating patterns.

Centralized Platform

Unify creation, taking, and tracking of assessments into one interface. Single login, single dashboard—eliminates navigation between multiple platforms.

AI-Assisted Generation (RAG)

Generate assessments directly from uploaded lecture materials using Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Ensures context-aware questions with minimal faculty effort.

Real-Time Proctoring

Automatically detects and logs cheating patterns including tab switching and unauthorized copy-pasting. Restores trust in online assessment integrity.

6. PRISM Platform Features

Centralized Assessment Platform

Unify creation, taking, and tracking of assessments into a single interface. Students navigate one platform for all course assessments, eliminating platform fragmentation and reducing cognitive load.

AI-Assisted Question Generation (RAG)

Upload lecture materials and PRISM automatically generates curriculum-based assessment questions using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Context-aware questions with minimal faculty effort—dramatically reduces question engineering workload.

Real-Time Proctoring with Default Tab-Locking

Intelligent monitoring automatically detects suspicious behaviors including tab switching, unauthorized copy-pasting, and other cheating indicators. All incidents logged with comprehensive audit trails. Default browser tab-locking prevents examination navigation.

Real-Time Analytics & Performance Monitoring

Comprehensive dashboards identify student knowledge gaps and learning patterns. Faculty receive actionable insights to provide targeted interventions and personalized support. Longitudinal performance tracking eliminates scattered data.

Automated Grading & Feedback

Instant assessment grading reduces faculty workload and provides immediate feedback to students. Automated rubric-based evaluation ensures consistency and fairness across all assessments.

7. Recommendations for Implementation

Official Platform Deployment

Gordon College CCS should officially adopt PRISM to replace the scattered ecosystem of third-party tools. Deploy on a dedicated, scalable virtual private server (DigitalOcean, AWS, or GCP) to guarantee zero downtime during concurrent departmental examinations.

Structured Faculty Training Program

Implement dedicated technical onboarding workshops to integrate PRISM's AI generation and data management features into standard faculty testing workflows. Training should align with the high student readiness metric to ensure faculty competency.

Institutional Infrastructure Support

Ensure stable internet connectivity within faculty rooms and laboratories. Stable internet infrastructure is critical for the system's cloud-based architecture and real-time proctoring to function without latency issues.

Continuous AI & Infrastructure Optimization

Evolve the prototype into a market-ready product through continuous optimization of AI tracking capabilities. Future versions should explore advanced monitoring for secondary devices and consider deployment of a dedicated mobile application variant.

8. Conclusion

Based on comprehensive quantitative survey findings from 317 respondents (298 students and 19 faculty), PRISM successfully demonstrates the viability of a centralized AI-driven assessment platform to address critical institutional challenges at Gordon College College of Computer Studies.

Key Conclusions:

  • Platform Fragmentation Validated: Current scattered approach creates significant cognitive load and technostress (WM = 3.34, "Agree")
  • Academic Integrity Crisis: Faculty identify academic integrity as the absolute highest concern (WM = 3.84, "Strongly Agree")
  • High Perceived Usefulness: Stakeholders recognize significant benefits of centralized AI-driven system (WM = 3.28)
  • Exceptional Technical Readiness: Faculty report zero deployment barriers (WM = 3.73, "Strongly Agree")
  • Strong Adoption Likelihood: Students and faculty demonstrate high willingness to transition to unified platform

PRISM represents a comprehensive solution grounded in quantitative research, cutting-edge MEAN stack technology, and adherence to rigorous development standards. The platform directly addresses platform fragmentation, reduces faculty workload through AI-assisted question generation and automated grading, restores academic integrity through real-time proctoring, and enables data-driven student support through advanced analytics. With exceptional stakeholder acceptance metrics and zero identified technical barriers, PRISM is positioned for successful institutional deployment and serves as a replicable model for educational institutions worldwide.
